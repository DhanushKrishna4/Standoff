Under the hood

How it actually decides

Most "group pickers" average everyone's ratings. That quietly rewards the blandest option and lets one grump tank the vote. This does something more careful.

Why not just average? Averaging is utilitarian — it maximises the total, which is how you end up watching the film everyone finds "fine" instead of the one three people love and one can live with. For a shared couch, the goal isn't the highest average — it's that nobody has a bad night.

  1. Feasibility first. A veto is a hard no — that genre is gone. Runtime caps filter too, and only stretch if the menu would otherwise be empty.
  2. Everyone on the same scale. Each person's score is normalised against their own range, so a harsh rater can't dominate a generous one by using different numbers.
  3. Fair, not just popular. Options are judged on the floor (how happy the least-keen person is — weighted highest), overall welfare, the Nash balance, and head-to-head wins (a Condorcet check).
  4. Memory across nights. Whoever falls behind on lifetime satisfaction is owed; next time it's close, the tie tips toward them.
  5. Honest tie-breaks. A true dead heat says "coin flip" rather than inventing a reason.

The five-method cross-check

It also runs the pick past the five canonical aggregation methods — each a different idea of fairness. When four or five agree, that's about as objective as group choice gets. When they split, or there's no Condorcet winner at all (a genuine cycle), it shows you the disagreement.

Is it solid — and could it be gamed?

Every verdict is stress-tested: would any single change of heart flip it, and could anyone get a better night by misreporting their taste? It checks each time and tells you the truth — including when the head-to-head favourite isn't the fair pick.
